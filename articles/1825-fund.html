December 2025

why 1825 fund exists

i grew up in a small town and loved building things: hardware, software, anything i could figure out.

what i didn't have was access.

no strong network. no mentors around me. and experimenting with hardware was expensive.

when i was around 13, during COVID, i taught myself whatever i could. i built random projects like a website that got 30k users, small games in unity for my girlfriend, experiments with a 3d printer just to see what was possible. none of it was polished. it was just curiosity.

i was lucky. i eventually found my way.

a lot of kids don't.

back then, i promised myself one thing:

if i ever had the chance, i wouldn't let lack of access be the reason someone gives up.

before there was a fund

before the 1825 fund had a name, i just started showing up.

i hosted small, free workshops in jhansi. i invited teenagers, many complete beginners, and taught them how to build their first website, how the internet works, and how to think about tech and startups.

one time, i shared a simple event link in a few whatsapp groups and on reddit.

18 people showed up. 14 of them were complete strangers.

by the end of the day, they had websites live on the internet. later, we all went out for pizza, paid for it ourselves, because effort deserves celebration.

later, i organized daydream jhansi, a 24-hour game jam for students aged 13-18. they built games from scratch, published them online, and kept everything open source. everyone got food. no entry fees. no pressure.

a 16-year-old built my favorite game that weekend.

check it out: https://shre-linux.itch.io/daydream

that's when it clicked.

why 1825 fund exists

if you're 16-22 in india and genuinely building something:

  • you're too early for VCs
  • too ambitious for parents
  • too unknown for grants

good ideas don't fail because they're bad.

they fail because no one backs them early enough.

1825 fund exists to be that first signal of belief.

we give small, fast, no-equity grants to young builders under 25.

no pitch decks. no degrees. no revenue requirements.

just: show us what you're already building.

our first fellows

we started small, using personal funds.

manan (16), ASTRA-V

manan is 16 and building ASTRA-V, a vertical landing rocket project.

when i asked him what the biggest challenge was, he didn't say exams or time. he said he's trying to figure out the fuel system, how to make it reliable enough so the rocket doesn't fail mid-air.

hearing that from a 16-year old really put things in perspective.

manan recently told us that version 1 of the flight controller is ready and will be tested in the coming days.

vishesh (17), nano mail

vishesh built nano mail, an open-source disposable email service focused on privacy.

it's live. people use it. and it was built entirely by a high-school student.

we funded vishesh to help with hosting, scaling, and improving the product so it could keep running.

what we're learning

money helps, but belief matters more.

we're also in conversation with programs like Z Fellows, where strong 1825 fund fellows may be fast-tracked for interviews, based on the quality of their work.

the goal isn't just funding.

it's visibility, confidence, and access to our network.

emergent ventures

recently, i received a grant from emergent ventures to continue doing this work and fund many more young builders.

it also means i'm moving to bangalore to be closer to the startup ecosystem and help more people.

this is still very early. we're learning as we go. we'll make mistakes. we'll get better.

but if even one 17-year-old from a small town keeps building instead of giving up because of this, it's worth it.

that's all this is.
