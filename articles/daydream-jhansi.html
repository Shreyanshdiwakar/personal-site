daydream jhansi

honestly? i just wanted to see what would happen if you gave a bunch of teenagers 24 hours, unlimited maggi, and told them to make a game.

that's basically how daydream jhansi started.

the idea was simple — get students aged 13 to 18, put them in a room together, and let them build whatever game they wanted. no restrictions. no "you should make this type of game." just... build something cool.

i wasn't sure if anyone would even show up. jhansi is not exactly known for game dev or hackathons. but i made a website anyway, posted it everywhere, and hoped for the best.

then the signups started coming in.

150+ people signed up. i was like... bhai kya ho raha hai.

most of them had never made a game before. some had never even coded before. but they showed up anyway — with their laptops, their friends, and zero sleep the night before because they were too excited.

to keep it accessible, we made the whole thing completely free. free entry, free food, free chai, unlimited snacks. because let's be real — if you're asking teenagers to stay awake for 24 hours straight, you better feed them properly.

the energy was insane. teams were forming, people were learning godot and unity on the spot, random strangers became friends, and by hour 6, the room was basically chaos in the best way possible.

everyone who finished and published their game on itch.io got stickers and merch. we also did community voting to pick the best games — winners got actual prizes.

one of my favourite projects was made by this 16-year-old kid. he'd never made a game before. 24 hours later, he had a fully working game on the internet:

  • itch.io: https://lnkd.in/g3rYdz_X
  • github: https://lnkd.in/gGT6A7Ap

all the games are open source now. anyone can play them, fork them, or build on top of them.

now here's the crazy part — funding this thing.

i had no sponsors. no connections. just vibes and a lot of cold messages.

i literally just dm'd the CTO of Zerodha on twitter. told him what we were doing. and he said yes.

in the end, we raised $2,000 USD in prize money. sponsors included FOSS United, Hack Club, Z Fellows, and a bunch of other people who believed in what we were trying to do.

looking back, daydream jhansi showed me something important — there's so much untapped talent in small towns. these kids just need someone to say "here's the space, here's the resources, now go build something."

a lot of participants shipped their first real project that day. for some, it was the first time anyone told them their work mattered.

that felt better than any prize money.
